The players

Every actor in this conflict has a calculation. Those calculations shape what happens next as much as anything inside Iran.

🇺🇸 United States · Trump
What it wants
A fast, decisive outcome before November 3, 2026: the midterm elections. All 435 House seats are contested. Republicans hold a narrow majority (218–214). Historically, the president's party loses an average of 26 House seats in midterms. A prolonged, expensive war would hand Democrats exactly the argument they need.
The tension
Trump ran on ending foreign entanglements. The war depletes US interceptor missile stockpiles, disrupts global energy, and risks inflation returning. If oil prices stay high, inflation comes back. And inflation is what cost Democrats the 2024 election. He knows this.
The calculation
Before summer: willing to escalate. After September: strong pressure toward any deal he can sell as a win. The midterm clock is running.
Nash position
Trump's dominant strategy shifts as November 3 approaches. The window for escalation closes by late summer.
🇮🇱 Israel
What it wants
Permanent elimination of Iran's nuclear program and degradation of IRGC capacity. Khamenei's death achieved the maximum target. Now the question is whether the successor regime reconstitutes the threat.
The calculation
Israel has no midterm election pressure. It can afford a longer timeline than the US. Washington may want out by September — Tel Aviv may want to keep pressure on until the succession crisis resolves in a direction it prefers. This creates potential divergence between the two allies.
Nash position
Use the US window while it lasts. Every week of ceasefire is a week Iran can reorganise. Israel has incentive to find reasons to resume if the ceasefire does not produce verifiable dismantlement.
🇷🇺 Russia
What it wants
The war to continue as long as possible without direct Russian involvement. Before the conflict, Russia's oil export earnings had fallen below $10 billion in February 2026. The Strait of Hormuz crisis sent prices sharply up — Russia earns an estimated $45–151 billion in additional revenue in 2026 (KSE Institute). Every day the US is in Iran is a day it is not focused on Ukraine.
The calculation
Silence, verbal condemnation, financial benefit, no escalation. China and Russia vetoed the UN Security Council resolution on Hormuz. The ceasefire is bad news for Moscow — oil prices fell 16% the day it was announced.
Nash position
Prolongation without involvement. Russia has no incentive to help end the war.
🇨🇳 China
What it wants
Stability in the Strait of Hormuz: 45% of its oil imports pass through it. Iran supplies ~13% of China's oil at below-market prices under a 25-year $400 billion agreement signed in 2021. China stockpiled aggressively in January–February 2026 (imports up 16%), building ~104 days of reserves.
The tension
China benefits from US distraction: every month Washington is in the Middle East, Beijing gains time in the Taiwan Strait without scrutiny. But a prolonged Hormuz closure harms the Chinese economy. A 25% rise in oil prices reduces China's GDP by ~0.5%.
The calculation
China wants the conflict to simmer, not boil. It has been supplying Iran with drone components and dual-use technology through the "Axis of Evasion" supply chain, while publicly presenting itself as a neutral peace mediator. The Trump-Xi summit, originally scheduled for April, has been postponed to May.
Nash position
Strategic ambiguity: support Iran enough to keep it functional as a US drain, not enough to be drawn in directly.
🛢️ Saudi Arabia and Gulf states
What they want
High oil prices, but not so high they crash global demand. Iran's oil off the market benefits Saudi Arabia directly. Before the conflict, Saudi Arabia was second-largest supplier to China (14.9%). Now it has room to expand market share.
The tension
Saudi Arabia and Gulf states fear Iranian retaliation: Bahrain closed its airspace, Kuwait suspended flights. The Strait of Hormuz passes through their neighbourhood. A prolonged conflict that damages Gulf infrastructure is not in their interest.
Nash position
A short war with high prices and Iranian oil permanently off the market. A ceasefire that restores Iranian production is bad for their market position.
🇪🇺 Europe
What it wants
The war to end quickly. European carriers (Lufthansa suspended Tehran flights until October 2026) face massive rerouting costs. Europe-Asia flights add 3–6 hours and significant fuel costs when avoiding Middle East airspace.
The calculation
Europe has no leverage. It opposes the war diplomatically but depends on US security. Its main tool is economic pressure — and Iran is already under maximum sanctions. Europe's role is peripheral.
Nash position
Push for ceasefire publicly while doing nothing that would damage US relations. A spectator with loud opinions.
The Nash equilibrium of the external game

Each player's dominant strategy points in a different direction. The US wants a fast exit before November. Israel wants sustained pressure. Russia wants prolongation. China wants controlled ambiguity. Saudi Arabia wants Iranian oil off the market. No single actor controls the outcome. The ceasefire of April 7 satisfies none of them fully. That is both its fragility and its function: a pause, not an equilibrium.

04

The system

Jack Goldstone studied 500 years of revolutions and found three conditions appear together in nearly every case of state collapse: elite defection, multi-class coalition, fiscal crisis. Iran in April 2026 has all three — partially and simultaneously. That has never happened before in its history. The question is whether they converge faster than the regime adapts.

The Islamic Republic survived the death of its supreme leader, the loss of its proxy network, economic collapse, mass protests, and external strikes. That is not luck. It was engineered.

Three interlocking layers

Layer 01
Religious legitimacy
The Supreme Leader, the Assembly of Experts (88 senior clerics), and the Guardian Council which vets every candidate for office. When Khamenei was killed, the Assembly moved within 8 days to appoint Mojtaba. The mechanism worked exactly as designed. Not despite the assassination, but through it.
Layer 02
The IRGC — military and economic empire
Not just a military force. The IRGC controls an estimated 50%+ of Iran's GDP through bonyads: revolutionary foundations turned corporate conglomerates in construction, mining, telecoms, real estate, and smuggling. A 2004 constitutional amendment allowed IRGC-affiliated organisations to control up to 80% of major economic sectors. If the regime falls, commanders lose everything and face criminal prosecution. This is not ideology. It is survival calculus.
Layer 03
The bureaucracy
The government, presidency, judiciary, and administrative apparatus that keeps daily functions running regardless of who sits at the top. This prevented collapse after Khamenei's death — the state continued at the operational level while succession was resolved at the theological level.

These layers overlap: IRGC officers sit in parliament. Clerics lead military ideological bureaus. No single shock can take down all three simultaneously.

What makes Iran different

Barbara Geddes (UCLA) built the largest dataset of authoritarian regime types. Her finding: regime type predicts durability more than any other factor.

Levitsky and Way (Harvard/Toronto): regimes born from violent social revolution are the most durable of all authoritarian types. Their founding destroys alternative power centres and forges institutions specifically designed to prevent reversal.

The mohareb mechanism

The regime calls protesters mohareb — enemies of God. Not a political label. A religious one. It means killing them is not only allowed but required by faith. Senior cleric Ali Saeedi said it out loud: defending the Islamic government "justifies the deaths of thousands." So when a soldier gets the order to fire, there is no internal voice saying this is wrong. Religion already answered that question. This is why 2019 killed 500+ people in days. And why 2022 went on for months. The moral brake that makes soldiers hesitate in other regimes has been removed.

The China comparison: the CCP held power since 1949 by doing what Iran cannot — separating economic growth from ideology. Deng Xiaoping freed the economy while keeping political control. The regime bought legitimacy through results. 800 million lifted from poverty. Iran went the opposite direction: sanctions and IRGC economic capture destroyed the middle class. No growth to offer. Only ideology and fear.

The opposition with no face

Every opposition movement that succeeded in the 20th century had a face. Mandela. Walesa. Havel. Khomeini himself. Iran's opposition today has none. No organisation, no unified leadership, no agreed programme for what comes after. This is partly by design — the regime imprisoned or exiled everyone who tried to lead.

What remains is harder to classify: a generation, young, urban, educated, female-led, that refuses the entire framework of the Islamic Republic, not just its current leaders. They don't want reform. They want a different country. But they can't coordinate because coordination requires communication, and communication is cut the moment protests begin.

The weakness
A leaderless movement can't negotiate, can't present demands, can't give the regime an off-ramp. That's why 2022 produced no political result despite being the largest uprising since 1979.
The strange strength
A movement with no head can't be decapitated. Arrest 50,000 people and it still exists, distributed across millions of private decisions not to comply, not to pretend, not to perform loyalty.

Why during a war? IRGC Chief Justice Mohseni-Ejei stated explicitly: "If we want to do a job, we should do it now — if it becomes late, two months, three months later, it doesn't have the same effect." The executions are timed while the world watches missiles and bombs. The regime knows the war will end. It's trying to make sure the population is too afraid to continue when it does.

The factor most analysis misses

The Islamic Republic is not one man. It is a system. Removing the man, even the supreme leader, does not remove the system. What removes the system is when the people inside it no longer believe it is worth defending. That is a process of decades, not of strikes. The generation that chanted "Woman, Life, Freedom" understood this intuitively. They were not trying to kill anyone. They were trying to make the system impossible to administer.

Iran since 1979

Each moment in the timeline shows what the factors looked like and what happened next.

1979
The Islamic Revolution
The Shah's rapid modernisation alienated clergy and bazaari merchants simultaneously. Oil wealth had created deep inequality. Carter's human rights pressure weakened repression signals. A cross-class coalition: clergy, leftists, liberals, unified against a single target. Crucially: the military refused orders to fire on protesters.
Economic inequality Cross-class coalition Military defection Full collapse
Today: economic grievances are higher than in 1979. But the IRGC has structural incentives the Shah's army did not, they own businesses, control smuggling routes. Their loyalty is bought, not conscripted.
1999
Student uprising
Student dormitories raided by Ansar Hezbollah paramilitary. Protests spread to 18 cities within days. The IRGC issued a direct letter warning they would "end the political game." Students had no broader coalition. Workers, clergy, and merchants did not join. Suppressed within a week.
Student-led only IRGC direct threat No coalition Suppressed
Key lesson: breadth of coalition matters more than intensity of protest. One class alone cannot tip the equilibrium.
2009
The Green Movement
Disputed presidential election: Mousavi against Ahmadinejad. Millions in the streets. The largest protests since 1979. International attention. The regime used Basij militia and internet blackouts. Leaders Mousavi and Karroubi placed under house arrest. They remain there to this day.
Millions mobilised International attention No security defections Suppressed
Had leadership and mass numbers, both factors Kuran's model predicts matter. But zero defections inside the security apparatus. The IRGC did not waver once.
2019
Bloody November
Fuel price tripled overnight. Spontaneous protests erupted across 29 provinces within 24 hours, faster than any previous movement. Internet cut for 5 days. Security forces killed over 1,500 protesters, according to a Reuters investigation. No organised leadership, no coordination possible.
Economic trigger 29 provinces simultaneous 1,500+ killed Internet blackout Suppressed
The regime will use lethal force at scale. The internet blackout is a primary tool, not a side effect. It prevents the coordination that Kuran's model requires for preference revelation to cascade.
2022–23
Woman, Life, Freedom
Mahsa Amini, 22, Kurdish, died in morality police custody September 16. Protests erupted the same day across all 31 provinces. Schoolgirls at the forefront, removing hijabs publicly. The slogan "Woman, Life, Freedom" directly challenged theocratic rule for the first time since 1979. Spread across ethnic lines: Kurds, Baluchis, Azeris, Persians. UN Fact-Finding Mission confirmed Iran responsible and found the crackdown constituted crimes against humanity. Over 500 killed, 22,000+ detained. At least 10 executed in connection with the protests as of 2024.
Cross-ethnic Women-led Anti-theocracy slogan 500+ killed · UN confirmed 22,000+ detained
The most significant movement since 1979. What was missing: no organised strike wave from oil workers (who brought down the Shah), no IRGC defections. The two structural factors Goldstone identifies as necessary were absent.
Dec 2025, Jan 2026
The January uprising
Started December 28, 2025 when bazaar traders in Tehran's Grand Bazaar closed shops after a sharp currency collapse. For the first time since 1979, the merchant class joined the protests. Spread to all 31 provinces and 220+ cities. On January 8–9, security forces carried out mass killings across Iran. The internet was cut completely on January 8, at the exact moment the killing reached its peak, making independent documentation nearly impossible. IHRNGO confirmed a minimum of 3,428 killed based on hospital and morgue documentation cross-referenced with two independent sources per case. HRANA documented 7,007 deaths by name. Local health officials cited by Time, The Guardian, and Iran International reported between 30,000 and 36,500 killed in those two days. Some estimates reach 40,000. The Iranian government has published no figures. 51,790 people were arrested (HRANA). Bodies were taken from hospitals by security forces. Families were prevented from burying their dead. Mass burials were reported. Doctors who treated wounded protesters were arrested.
Bazaar traders joined, first since 1979 3,400–40,000 killed 51,790 arrested 220+ cities 31 provinces Internet cut Jan 8
Why such a wide range: the internet blackout was imposed precisely when the killing was most intense. Bodies were removed before they could be counted. The lower figure (3,428) is what could be documented despite everything. The higher figures (30,000–40,000) come from health officials inside Iran who had access to morgue data before it was seized. Both the low and the high numbers are significant. The regime's refusal to publish any figure is itself a figure.

What the timeline adds up to

Jack Goldstone studied 500 years of revolutions and found three conditions appear together in nearly every case of state collapse.

Elite defection
Security commanders stop following orders. Not private doubt — visible, irreversible public acts.
Partially triggered
Mojtaba Khamenei named March 8. Never appeared in person — statements read by TV anchor. Intel memos cite him as incapacitated in Qom. Real power held by IRGC wartime council of Rezaei, Vahidi, Ghalibaf.
Multi-class coalition
Workers, merchants, and middle class in the streets simultaneously. 1979 had all three.
Developing
Bazaar traders joined Jan 2026 — first since 1979. Suppressed. But the economic conditions that brought them out remain.
Fiscal crisis
The regime cannot pay its security forces. The IRGC's loyalty becomes unaffordable.
Acute
Oil revenues compressed by war and sanctions. Not yet breaking — IRGC still paid through evasion. But the margin is narrowing.

All three conditions are now present in partial form simultaneously. That has never happened before in the history of the Islamic Republic. Each of the six moments in this timeline produced one or two conditions. None produced all three at once. April 2026 is the first time all three are active together — even if none is fully developed. The question is not whether they can converge. It is whether they converge faster than the regime adapts.

On the numbers: ranges, not single figures

Every death toll, arrest figure, and economic statistic on this site comes with a range rather than a single number. This is not evasion. It is honesty.

The Iranian government does not publish casualty figures, protest arrest numbers, or execution counts that can be independently verified. It controls access to hospitals, morgues, and prisons. It cuts the internet at the moment repression intensifies, not by accident, but as a documented deliberate strategy to prevent documentation.

The lower figures on this site (for example, 3,428 killed in January 2026) represent what human rights organisations could document by name, with two independent sources per case, through the blackout. The higher figures (30,000–40,000) come from health officials inside Iran who had access to institutional data before it was seized. Both are real. The truth is somewhere in between, and the regime has made it impossible to know where.

When a government kills people and then makes it impossible to count them, the count itself becomes an act of resistance. HRANA, IHRNGO, Amnesty International, and Human Rights Watch do this work under extreme conditions. These numbers are the most rigorous available — not because they are certain, but because they are the most verified.

The models

Nash Equilibrium
Each actor — regime, IRGC, population — chooses the strategy best given what others are doing. The regime "holds" as long as the IRGC's best response is compliance. This shifts when the cost of compliance exceeds the cost of defection: depends on fiscal capacity and perceived future safety.
Kuran — Preference Falsification (1995)
People hide their real preferences under authoritarian regimes. When enough people privately disagree but publicly comply, a single trigger can cascade into mass mobilisation faster than any model predicts. The "hidden threshold" in the simulator estimates this gap.
Goldstone — Structural-Demographic Theory
Three structural conditions precede nearly all historical state collapses: elite defection, multi-class coalition, fiscal crisis. Used here to score the presence or absence of each precondition.
Bueno de Mesquita — Selectorate Theory
A regime survives by paying a "winning coalition" — in Iran's case: the IRGC and its business network, ~$12–20bn annually from oil and smuggling. When revenue falls below the cost of maintaining this coalition, the coalition has incentive to defect.

What this can and cannot do

No model can tell you what will happen. The researchers who built the best political science models have predicted revolutions no better than chance. One soldier who refuses an order. One video that reaches the right person. Any of these can change everything.

What this does is show the structure. The forces in play. The historical parallels. The conditions that came before change in the past, and whether they are present now. Not comfort. Something real.
